# Geopolitical Risk — Cable Assembly Subsystem Layer

## Teaser
The Baltic Sea cable cuts prove that submarine infrastructure is a target — and HMN Technologies' exclusion reshapes the competitive map

## Title
State ownership of submarine cable manufacturers, deliberate cable sabotage, and the bifurcation of the global cable network into western and Chinese spheres define the subsystem layer's geopolitical risk

## Category
Geopolitical risk

---

## Overview

Submarine cables carry 99% of intercontinental data traffic. This makes the subsystem layer a geopolitical asset of the first order — and a target. The past three years have exposed three distinct risk vectors: physical sabotage of submarine infrastructure, the exclusion of Chinese manufacturers from western cable projects, and the emergence of parallel cable networks serving different geopolitical blocs. Each vector is accelerating.

## HMN Technologies and the bifurcation of cable markets

HMN Technologies (formerly Huawei Marine Networks, rebranded after Hengtong acquired Huawei's 51% stake in 2019) held approximately 23% of the global submarine cable market by length installed. Despite the ownership restructuring to remove the Huawei name, western governments and hyperscalers have systematically excluded HMN from new cable projects on national security grounds.

The US government has actively steered submarine cable contracts toward SubCom, the only US-owned manufacturer of repeatered submarine cable systems. The 2020 Team Telecom recommendation against the Pacific Light Cable Network (PLCN) — which would have connected the US to Hong Kong via a cable partially supplied by HMN — set the precedent for excluding Chinese-linked manufacturers from cables touching US territory.

This exclusion has not eliminated HMN from the market. Instead, it has bifurcated the global submarine cable network into two spheres. HMN continues to win contracts in Belt and Road Initiative markets: the PEACE cable (Pakistan & East Africa Connecting Europe, approximately 25,000 km) connects Pakistan, East Africa, and Europe; the 2Africa cable extension added segments with Chinese manufacturing participation; and new systems connecting Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and Africa frequently include HMN as a supplier.

The practical consequence: the submarine cable industry now operates two parallel supply chains. Western-funded systems (hyperscaler projects, NATO-aligned government cables) use SubCom, ASN, or NEC exclusively. Chinese and Belt and Road systems use HMN and Hengtong. The overlap zone — cables serving both western and Chinese endpoints — creates procurement complexity that adds cost and timeline risk.

## Baltic Sea cable sabotage

Since 2022, at least 10 submarine cable and pipeline incidents have occurred in the Baltic Sea, ranging from anchor-drag damage (attributed to commercial shipping, some suspected deliberate) to the Nord Stream pipeline sabotage. The October 2023 damage to the Balticconnector gas pipeline and the Estonia-Finland telecom cable within 24 hours demonstrated the vulnerability of co-located submarine infrastructure.

NATO responded by launching a Maritime Centre for the Security of Critical Undersea Infrastructure and deploying surveillance assets to monitor submarine cable routes in the Baltic and North Sea. Multiple NATO members have increased naval patrols along critical cable corridors.

The cable sabotage risk has practical implications for the subsystem layer's investment case. Repair operations require specialized CLVs — the same vessels needed for new cable installation. Every repair mobilization displaces scheduled installation work, compounding the vessel bottleneck. Cable repair costs range from $1–3 million per incident for shallow-water damage to $10+ million for deep-water breaks requiring extended vessel deployment.

Insurance costs for submarine cable systems have increased 20–30% since 2022, reflecting both sabotage risk and the increasing replacement value of modern high-capacity systems. Some insurers now require specific route risk assessments for cables transiting contested waters.

## France nationalizes ASN

In March 2024, the French government completed the acquisition of Alcatel Submarine Networks (ASN) from Nokia for approximately €350 million. The nationalization was explicitly framed as a national security decision — France determined that sovereign control of submarine cable manufacturing capability was strategically essential.

ASN manufactures submarine cable systems at its Calais factory and has historically been a leading provider of systems connecting Europe to Africa, the Middle East, and Asia. Under state ownership, ASN's project selection may increasingly reflect French and EU strategic priorities rather than purely commercial considerations. This could mean preferential allocation of ASN capacity to European digital sovereignty projects (such as the EU's planned submarine cable network to reduce dependency on US hyperscaler-controlled systems) at the expense of commercial availability for non-European buyers.

## US government influence on SubCom

SubCom, acquired by Cerberus Capital Management in 2018 for approximately $325 million, is the only US-owned manufacturer of repeatered submarine cable systems. The US government has used diplomatic channels to steer cable contracts toward SubCom, particularly in the Indo-Pacific region where submarine cable routes are strategically significant.

The Defense Production Act Title III program — which awarded $18.1 million to 5N Plus for germanium production — reflects the broader US government approach to treating the entire fiber supply chain as defense-relevant infrastructure. SubCom's Newington, New Hampshire facility produces cable for both commercial and government/military applications.

## Submarine cable route security

Cable route selection has become a geopolitical exercise. New cables increasingly avoid transit through contested waters (South China Sea, Taiwan Strait, Red Sea) or add redundant segments to reduce single-point-of-failure risk. The Equiano cable (Google, West Africa to Europe) and the Raman cable (Google, US to India via Jordan) were explicitly routed to avoid choke points.

Cable landing stations in strategically sensitive locations — Djibouti, Suez, Singapore, Guam — are subject to increasing government oversight. The US-Congo mineral deal (February 2026) included provisions for submarine cable infrastructure development, linking raw material access to digital infrastructure investment.

## Takeaway

The subsystem layer's geopolitical risks are simultaneously the most visible (Baltic cable cuts generate international headlines) and the most structurally significant (market bifurcation reshapes the competitive landscape for decades). The nationalization of ASN, the effective exclusion of HMN from western projects, and the US government's strategic support for SubCom have created a submarine cable industry where geopolitical alignment is a prerequisite for market access. For investors, this means the competitive dynamics of the subsystem layer are shaped as much by government policy as by technology or cost.
