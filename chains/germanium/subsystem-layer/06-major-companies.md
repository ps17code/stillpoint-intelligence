# Major Companies — Cable Assembly Subsystem Layer

## Teaser
Prysmian's $31B market cap, €16–17B backlog, and 8-vessel fleet make it the subsystem layer's dominant integrated player — but the real pricing power sits with SubCom's wet-plant monopoly in the US

## Title
Six companies control the subsystem layer: Prysmian (integrated cable-to-vessel), SubCom (US wet-plant), ASN (French state-owned), NEC (Trans-Pacific), Dycom (terrestrial deployment), and the excluded HMN Technologies (Belt and Road)

## Category
Major companies

## Overview

The subsystem layer is controlled by a small number of companies, each occupying a distinct competitive position. Unlike the component layer where Umicore and a few Chinese refiners dominate a single material, the subsystem layer's competitive landscape is segmented by geography, capability (wet-plant vs. cable-only vs. installation), and geopolitical alignment. Market position in the subsystem layer is determined primarily by installed base, qualification history, and vessel access — not by cost or technology alone.

## Prysmian Group — The integrated champion

**Market cap:** ~$31 billion (as of early 2026)
**Revenue:** €17.1 billion (FY2024)
**Submarine cable backlog:** €16–17 billion
**Headquarters:** Milan, Italy
**Key facilities:** Arco Felice (Naples) — world's largest submarine cable factory; Nordenham (Germany); Montereau (France); North American preform and cable plants
**Fleet:** 8 cable-laying vessels by 2028, including Leonardo da Vinci (commissioned 2024)

Prysmian is the world's largest cable company by revenue and the only player with full vertical integration from fiber preform manufacturing through cable production to vessel-based installation. The 2018 acquisition of General Cable and the organic expansion of its submarine division created a company that controls more submarine cable manufacturing capacity than any competitor.

The Leonardo da Vinci — 170m length, 10,000-tonne cable capacity, DP3 positioning — represents a step change in CLV capability. Prysmian's fleet expansion to 8 vessels gives it scheduling independence: the company can prioritize its own projects without competing for third-party vessel time.

**Competitive position:** Prysmian's integrated model (factory + fleet) creates structural advantages in project execution and timeline certainty. The backlog of €16–17 billion provides revenue visibility through 2028+. The key risk is execution — managing 8 vessels and multiple factories across simultaneous mega-projects requires operational discipline that Prysmian's historical track record on submarine projects has sometimes lacked.

## SubCom — The American wet-plant monopoly

**Ownership:** Cerberus Capital Management (acquired 2018 for ~$325 million)
**Headquarters:** Newington, New Hampshire
**Employees:** ~1,800
**Key capabilities:** Repeatered submarine cable systems, wet-plant manufacturing, cable installation

SubCom is the only US-owned manufacturer of repeatered submarine cable systems. This unique position makes it the de facto supplier for US government-affiliated projects, cables landing on US territory where national security review (Team Telecom) favors domestic suppliers, and hyperscaler projects where US-based relationship management is preferred.

SubCom manufactured the MAREA cable (Microsoft-Meta-Telxius, Virginia Beach to Bilbao), the Dunant cable (Google, Virginia Beach to Saint-Hilaire-de-Riez), and numerous other high-profile systems. Its Newington facility produces cable, repeaters, and branching units — the complete wet-plant stack.

**Competitive position:** SubCom's value is primarily strategic rather than scale-based. Its US ownership and security clearances make it irreplaceable for defense-adjacent projects. Cerberus's acquisition at $325 million looks like one of the most successful private equity deals in telecom infrastructure — the company's replacement value today would be multiples of that price. The risk is private equity ownership: Cerberus may seek an exit (IPO or strategic sale) that could disrupt customer relationships.

## ASN (Alcatel Submarine Networks) — The French sovereign asset

**Ownership:** French government (acquired from Nokia, March 2024, ~€350 million)
**Headquarters:** Calais, France
**Employees:** ~2,000
**Key capabilities:** Turnkey submarine cable systems, repeaters, cable manufacturing

ASN's nationalization transformed it from a Nokia division managed for portfolio optimization into a sovereign industrial asset managed for strategic capability. Under state ownership, ASN is expanding its Calais factory and investing in next-generation repeater technology, including SDM-capable designs.

ASN has historically been the leading supplier for submarine cable systems connecting Europe to Africa and the Middle East — the Ellalink cable (Europe-Brazil), SEA-ME-WE systems, and multiple Africa Coast to Europe cables were ASN-manufactured. This geographic franchise aligns with French and EU strategic interests in digital connectivity to francophone Africa and the Mediterranean.

**Competitive position:** State ownership provides capital access and strategic patience that commercial competitors cannot match. The risk is politicization of project selection — if ASN prioritizes sovereign projects over commercially optimal contracts, its market share in competitive tenders may decline. However, the constrained capacity environment through 2028 means ASN has more demand than it can serve regardless of strategic priorities.

## NEC Corporation — Submarine Networks Division

**Market cap:** ~$38 billion (parent company)
**Submarine division revenue:** Estimated $1.5–2 billion annually
**Headquarters:** Tokyo, Japan
**Key capabilities:** Repeatered submarine cable systems, Trans-Pacific specialization

NEC's submarine cable division is the smallest of the three western-aligned wet-plant manufacturers but holds a strong position in Trans-Pacific routes where Japanese government relationships and NEC's regional presence provide competitive advantages. NEC manufactured the Japan-US Cable Network (Unity), the SJC cable system, and numerous intra-Asian submarine systems.

NEC has invested in next-generation submarine technology, including multi-core fiber transmission research and AI-optimized repeater management systems. Its laboratory demonstrations of 319 Tb/s transmission on 20-core fiber position NEC as a technology leader even if its manufacturing scale is smaller than Prysmian or ASN.

**Competitive position:** NEC's position is defensible in Trans-Pacific and intra-Asian routes but limited in Atlantic and Africa-Europe corridors where ASN and SubCom dominate. The parent company's diversified technology portfolio means submarine cables receive investment attention proportional to their strategic importance to NEC's overall government-facing business.

## Dycom Industries — Terrestrial deployment pure play

**Market cap:** ~$17 billion
**Revenue:** $5.55 billion (FY2026 ended January 2026)
**Backlog:** $9.54 billion (up from $7.38 billion YoY)
**Headquarters:** Palm Beach Gardens, Florida
**Key capabilities:** Fiber optic cable placement, underground construction, aerial construction, splicing, testing

Dycom is the largest specialty contractor for terrestrial fiber optic cable deployment in North America. The company does not manufacture cable — it installs it. Dycom's customers are telcos (AT&T, Lumen, Verizon), cable operators (Charter, Comcast), and increasingly hyperscalers building campus and metro fiber networks.

The $9.54 billion backlog (up 29% YoY) reflects the convergence of BEAD construction, hyperscaler campus deployment, and 5G fiber backhaul. Dycom's FY2027 revenue guidance of $6.85–7.15 billion implies continued 20%+ growth. The company operates approximately 15,000 employees across 49 states.

**Competitive position:** Dycom's competitive advantage is workforce scale and training infrastructure. The company's ability to recruit, train, and deploy fiber construction crews at scale is the binding constraint on terrestrial fiber deployment — and Dycom controls the largest pool of this scarce labor. MasTec and Quanta Services compete in the space but have more diversified infrastructure portfolios.

## HMN Technologies — The excluded competitor

**Ownership:** Hengtong Group (51%) — formerly Huawei Marine Networks
**Headquarters:** Tianjin, China
**Market share:** ~23% of global submarine cable by km installed (pre-exclusion)
**Key capabilities:** Unrepeated and short-haul submarine cable, emerging repeatered capability

HMN Technologies remains a significant global player despite its exclusion from western-funded cable projects. The company continues to win contracts in Belt and Road markets and has been selected for submarine systems connecting Southeast Asia, Africa, and the Middle East where Chinese financing accompanies cable contracts.

HMN's technical capabilities have advanced since the Huawei divestiture, with the company developing repeatered cable systems that could challenge western manufacturers on routes where security review is not a factor. The PEACE cable system (25,000 km, Pakistan to France via East Africa and Egypt) is HMN's most prominent repeatered project.

**Competitive position:** HMN operates in a separate market from the three western manufacturers. Its exclusion from US, European, and allied-nation cable projects limits its addressable market to approximately 30–40% of global submarine cable demand. Within that market, HMN's cost advantage (estimated 20–30% below western competitors) and Chinese government financial support make it the dominant supplier.

## Takeaway

The subsystem layer's competitive structure is remarkably concentrated. Three companies (SubCom, ASN, NEC) control wet-plant manufacturing for the western-aligned market. One company (Prysmian) dominates integrated cable-to-installation delivery. One company (Dycom) controls the largest terrestrial deployment workforce. And one company (HMN) serves the parallel Chinese-aligned market. This concentration — reinforced by geopolitical alignment, qualification barriers, and vessel access — creates durable competitive advantages measured in decades rather than product cycles.
