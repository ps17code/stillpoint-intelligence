# Investment Ideas — Cable Assembly Subsystem Layer

## Teaser
Prysmian's integrated model, Dycom's labor moat, and the cable vessel scarcity thesis offer three distinct entry points — but the real asymmetry is SubCom's eventual IPO

## Title
Long Prysmian for integrated capacity, long Dycom for terrestrial deployment, watch for SubCom IPO as the decade's highest-conviction submarine infrastructure event — and short operators without vessel access or secured fiber supply

## Category
Investment ideas

## Overview

The subsystem layer offers investment opportunities across multiple time horizons and risk profiles. The common thesis underlying all positions is that submarine and terrestrial cable deployment capacity is structurally undersupplied relative to demand through at least 2028, creating pricing power for companies that control bottleneck assets: wet-plant manufacturing, cable laying vessels, and trained installation crews. Unlike the component layer where a binary Chinese export control decision dominates the risk profile, the subsystem layer's investment case rests on observable industrial constraints with multi-year resolution timelines.

## Idea 01 — Long Prysmian Group (BIT: PRY)

**Thesis:** Prysmian is the only company in the world with integrated submarine cable manufacturing, fiber preform production, and a captive CLV fleet. The €16–17 billion submarine backlog provides revenue visibility through 2028+. The Leonardo da Vinci and fleet expansion to 8 vessels eliminate the vessel scheduling risk that constrains competitors.

**Catalysts:** Quarterly backlog updates showing sustained demand. New cable system contract wins (each $200M–1B+). Fleet utilization data showing high vessel productivity. North American BABA-compliant preform capacity benefiting from BEAD demand.

**Valuation:** At ~$31 billion market cap on €17.1 billion revenue, Prysmian trades at roughly 1.8x revenue — modest for a company with this backlog visibility and competitive position. Submarine cables are the highest-margin segment (estimated 15–20% EBITDA margins vs. 10–12% for terrestrial cables).

**Risks:** Execution risk on simultaneous mega-projects. Offshore wind cable margin pressure if energy transition slows. Italian corporate governance complexity. Currency exposure (euro revenues, global cost base).

## Idea 02 — Long Dycom Industries (NYSE: DY)

**Thesis:** Dycom is the pure-play beneficiary of terrestrial fiber deployment acceleration. The $9.54 billion backlog (29% YoY growth) and $6.85–7.15 billion FY2027 revenue guidance reflect the convergence of BEAD, hyperscaler campus builds, and 5G backhaul. Dycom's labor moat — the largest trained fiber installation workforce in North America — is the scarcest resource in the deployment chain.

**Catalysts:** BEAD construction ramp through 2026–2028 as state subgrantee agreements convert to active construction. Hyperscaler campus fiber contracts (Meta-Corning $6B deal flows through installers like Dycom). Backlog growth acceleration as BEAD projects enter the construction phase. Margin expansion as crew utilization improves with larger project volumes.

**Valuation:** At ~$17 billion market cap on $5.55 billion revenue, Dycom trades at approximately 3x revenue — a premium reflecting the growth trajectory. The stock historically trades at 12–15x forward EBITDA. The multi-year demand visibility from BEAD supports a premium multiple.

**Risks:** BEAD program delays (political or administrative). Customer concentration (AT&T historically 20%+ of revenue). Weather and seasonal construction variability. Labor cost inflation if skilled worker shortage persists.

## Idea 03 — SubCom IPO watch

**Thesis:** Cerberus Capital acquired SubCom in 2018 for approximately $325 million. The company is now the sole US manufacturer of repeatered submarine cable systems, with order books filled through 2027 and strategic importance to the US government. A Cerberus exit via IPO would likely value SubCom at $5–10 billion based on comparable multiples (Prysmian submarine segment, defense contractors with strategic capabilities), representing a 15–30x return on the 2018 acquisition.

**Investment approach:** Monitor SEC filings for S-1 registration. Track Cerberus portfolio activity for exit signals. Position in Cerberus-affiliated vehicles if accessible. Prepare allocation for IPO participation.

**Catalysts:** Cerberus typically holds investments for 5–7 years — the 2018 acquisition puts an exit in the 2025–2027 window. The constrained capacity environment maximizes SubCom's valuation by demonstrating pricing power and strategic irreplaceability. US government defense spending increases could accelerate exit timing.

**Risks:** Cerberus may pursue strategic sale (to a defense prime like L3Harris or Leidos) rather than IPO, limiting public market access. Private equity exits in defense-adjacent businesses face CFIUS scrutiny, which could delay or condition a transaction.

## Idea 04 — Cable vessel operators and builders

**Thesis:** CLV scarcity creates pricing power for vessel operators and demand for new vessel construction. Day rates have increased 40–60% since 2023. The aging fleet (66% reaching end-of-life by 2040) requires replacement regardless of demand cycles.

**Investment approach:** This is primarily a private market or shipping equity thesis. Publicly traded shipping companies with CLV exposure include DEME Group (submarine cable installation services) and several Asian marine engineering firms. South Korean and Japanese shipyards building CLV newbuilds (Hyundai Heavy Industries, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries) benefit from the vessel replacement cycle.

**Catalysts:** Vessel day rate increases. New CLV orders. Prysmian fleet expansion announcements. Offshore wind cable installation demand competing for the same vessel pool.

**Risks:** Vessel construction timelines (3–4 years from order to delivery). Cyclical offshore energy demand could compete for or redirect vessels. Technology shifts (e.g., unmanned installation systems) could alter fleet economics, though this is a 2030+ risk.

## Idea 05 — Coherent optics vendors for submarine upgrades

**Thesis:** Open cable architectures enable terminal equipment upgrades independent of wet-plant changes. The transition from 400G to 800G to 1.6T wavelengths on existing cable systems creates recurring revenue for coherent optics vendors — each upgrade cycle multiplies cable capacity without new submarine construction.

**Companies:** Ciena (NYSE: CIEN), which supplies WaveLogic coherent transponders for multiple submarine systems. Infinera (acquired by Nokia, 2024) — submarine-grade coherent optics. Acacia Communications (acquired by Cisco) — high-baud-rate coherent DSP technology.

**Catalysts:** Open cable system adoption increasing (TIP OCS specification). 1.6T transponder commercial availability 2026–2027. Cable operators upgrading existing systems rather than building new ones (lower capex per bit).

**Risks:** Coherent optics is a competitive market with rapid technology cycles. Nokia-Infinera and Cisco-Acacia integrations create uncertainty. The submarine terminal equipment market is much smaller than the datacenter interconnect market that drives most coherent optics revenue.

## Idea 06 — Short: operators without secured fiber supply or vessel access

**Thesis:** Smaller submarine cable operators and terrestrial ISPs without long-term fiber supply agreements or vessel access face margin compression as input costs rise and construction capacity remains scarce. Companies relying entirely on spot-market fiber procurement and third-party vessel charter are most exposed.

**Indicators to watch:** Fiber procurement concentration (single-source vs. diversified). Charter vessel dependence vs. owned fleet. Backlog duration (shorter = more exposed to spot pricing). BABA compliance status (non-compliant manufacturers locked out of BEAD revenue).

**Risks:** Short positions in infrastructure companies carry execution risk — many of these companies are small-cap or private, with limited borrow availability. Government subsidies (BEAD, RDOF) may provide temporary relief that delays but does not prevent margin compression.

## Takeaway

The subsystem layer's investment case is built on observable physical constraints with multi-year resolution timelines. Prysmian (integrated capacity), Dycom (labor moat), and SubCom (strategic monopoly) offer the highest-conviction opportunities. The coherent optics upgrade cycle adds a technology-driven catalyst. The common risk across all positions is demand cyclicality — if hyperscaler capex slows or BEAD faces political headwinds, the capacity constraints that create pricing power today could become overcapacity risk in 2029–2030.
