# Supply Constraints — Cable Assembly Subsystem Layer

## Teaser
The world needs 60 new submarine cable systems this decade — and only three companies can build the wet plant that makes them work

## Title
Cable manufacturing capacity, vessel availability, and repeater production define a subsystem layer where physical constraints outweigh financial ones

## Category
Supply constraints

---

## Overview

The subsystem layer converts fiber optic components into deployed cable systems — submarine and terrestrial — that carry 99% of intercontinental data traffic. Unlike the component layer where a single material (germanium) creates a chokepoint, the subsystem layer faces simultaneous constraints across three interdependent production chains: cable manufacturing, wet-plant assembly (repeaters and branching units), and cable laying vessel availability. Each constraint reinforces the others. A manufacturer cannot deliver a cable system without repeaters; repeaters cannot be deployed without a laying vessel; and vessels cannot be built faster than 3–4 years regardless of demand.

## Submarine cable manufacturing

The global submarine cable market reached $32.8 billion in 2026 (projected), driven by hyperscaler demand for intercontinental bandwidth. Approximately 60 new submarine cable systems are planned or under construction globally through 2030. The three integrated manufacturers — SubCom, ASN (Alcatel Submarine Networks), and NEC — control more than 60% of wet-plant revenue and operate the only facilities capable of manufacturing repeatered submarine cable systems at scale.

Total submarine cable manufacturing capacity is estimated at approximately 100,000–120,000 km/year across all manufacturers globally. Against planned deployments requiring 400,000+ km of new submarine cable through 2030, the manufacturing pipeline is saturated. Lead times for turnkey submarine cable systems have stretched to 3–4 years from contract to ready-for-service, compared with 18–24 months historically.

Prysmian's submarine cable division operates the world's largest submarine cable factory in Arco Felice (Naples), Italy, with additional production at Nordenham (Germany) and Montereau (France). Order backlog for submarine telecom and power cables exceeded €16–17 billion as of early 2026. Prysmian commissioned 8 cable-laying vessels by 2028 — the largest private fleet in the industry.

## Terrestrial cable and fiber deployment

Terrestrial fiber deployment faces different but equally binding constraints. The US alone requires an estimated 213.3 million additional fiber miles by 2029 to meet BEAD program targets and hyperscaler campus connectivity demands. Fiber cable manufacturing capacity in North America has expanded — Corning's Hickory plant, Prysmian's North American facilities, and OFS (Furukawa subsidiary) — but trained splicing crews, conduit availability, and permitting bottlenecks limit deployment rates regardless of cable supply.

Single-mode fiber prices surged more than 500% between January 2025 and early 2026, with G.657A2 fiber reaching $35/km and Chinese G.652.D prices jumping from 22 yuan/core-km to 31.5 yuan/core-km (with spot transactions at 40–50 yuan). This pricing pressure reflects not just germanium input costs but genuine physical scarcity of finished cable inventory.

## Repeater and wet-plant constraints

EDFA (Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier) repeaters are the highest-value components in submarine cable systems — a single transoceanic system may require 100+ repeaters at $500K–1M+ each. Repeater order books at SubCom and ASN are filled through 2027. NEC's repeater capacity is similarly constrained, with the company prioritizing Trans-Pacific routes where it holds historical market position.

Branching units — the subsea switching nodes that allow cable systems to serve multiple landing points — require custom engineering for each deployment. Lead times for branching units have extended to 18–24 months, creating scheduling dependencies that cascade through entire project timelines.

## Cable laying vessel fleet

The global cable laying vessel (CLV) fleet comprises approximately 60 ships capable of submarine cable installation, but only 15–20 are rated for deep-water transoceanic work. The fleet is aging: approximately 66% of vessels will reach end-of-life by 2040 without replacement. Nine newbuild CLVs are scheduled for delivery by end 2026, including Prysmian's Leonardo da Vinci (the world's most advanced CLV, commissioned 2024) and several vessels ordered by Asian operators.

Vessel day rates have increased 40–60% since 2023, reflecting both demand pressure and the limited fleet size. Weather windows for installation in the North Atlantic and North Sea restrict laying seasons to approximately 200 operational days per year, creating scheduling bottlenecks that cannot be solved with additional capital.

## Takeaway

The subsystem layer's supply constraints are fundamentally different from the component layer's single-material bottleneck. Here, the constraints are distributed across manufacturing, equipment, vessels, and labor — each with its own expansion timeline measured in years, not months. The submarine cable industry's 3–4 year project cycles mean that capacity decisions made in 2024–2025 determine what is deliverable in 2028–2029. For terrestrial deployment, the binding constraint has shifted from cable availability to installation capacity — trained crews, permits, and conduit access.
