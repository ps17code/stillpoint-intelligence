# Bottlenecks — Cable Assembly Subsystem Layer

## Teaser
SubCom, ASN, and NEC control the wet-plant oligopoly — and their order books are full through 2027

## Title
Three chokepoints define the subsystem layer: wet-plant integration, cable laying vessel scheduling, and landing station permitting create irreducible lead times

## Category
Bottlenecks

---

## Overview

The subsystem layer's bottlenecks are structural rather than cyclical. Unlike commodity shortages that resolve with price increases, the constraints here involve specialized industrial capabilities that require years to replicate. Three interdependent bottlenecks — wet-plant manufacturing concentration, cable vessel scheduling, and regulatory permitting — create a minimum project timeline of 3–4 years that no amount of capital can compress below 2–3 years.

## Bottleneck 01 — Wet-plant oligopoly

SubCom (US, private/Cerberus Capital), ASN (France, state-owned), and NEC (Japan, public) collectively control more than 60% of submarine wet-plant revenue. Wet-plant refers to the active subsea components: EDFA repeaters, reconfigurable optical add-drop multiplexers (ROADMs), branching units, and power feed equipment. These are the highest-margin, most technically complex elements of a submarine cable system.

The barrier to entry is extreme. Wet-plant manufacturing requires decades of accumulated reliability data — submarine repeaters must operate for 25+ years at ocean-floor pressures without maintenance access. No new entrant has successfully broken into the repeatered cable market in over two decades. Hengtong and HMN Technologies (formerly Huawei Marine) produce unrepeated and short-haul submarine cable but lack the qualification history for transoceanic repeatered systems that western carriers and hyperscalers require.

Repeater order books at all three manufacturers are committed through 2027. SubCom's Newington, New Hampshire facility is running at maximum capacity. ASN's Calais factory — modernized after French government nationalization in 2024 — is expanding but constrained by skilled labor availability. NEC's submarine division in Japan serves primarily Trans-Pacific routes.

The practical implication: a hyperscaler placing a new transoceanic cable order today will wait 3–4 years for ready-for-service delivery. This is why Meta committed $10 billion+ to Project Waterworth (approximately 50,000 km of new submarine cable) as a multi-year program rather than a single procurement.

## Bottleneck 02 — Cable laying vessel scheduling

The global CLV fleet operates as a scheduling bottleneck independent of manufacturing capacity. Even when cable is manufactured and repeaters are ready, installation requires booking vessel time 12–18 months in advance. The constrained fleet creates cascading schedule risks: a single weather delay or equipment failure on one project pushes vessel availability for subsequent projects.

Critical scheduling factors include weather windows (North Atlantic operations limited to April–October for deep-water work), vessel transit time between project sites (2–4 weeks depending on route), and the specialized equipment requirements for different cable types (power cables require different laying equipment than telecom cables). The dual-use nature of many CLVs — capable of both telecom and offshore wind power cable installation — creates competition for vessel time between the submarine telecom and offshore energy sectors.

Prysmian has responded by building the industry's largest private CLV fleet (8 vessels by 2028), including the Leonardo da Vinci commissioned in 2024 with 10,000-tonne cable capacity and DP3 positioning. This vertical integration — factory to vessel to installation — gives Prysmian scheduling priority for its own projects but removes vessel capacity from the open market.

## Bottleneck 03 — Landing station permitting and construction

Every submarine cable system requires terrestrial landing stations at each endpoint — the facilities where subsea fiber transitions to terrestrial networks. Landing station permitting involves coastal environmental reviews, security assessments (particularly for cables near military installations), and local government approvals that collectively take 18–36 months in the US and 12–24 months in most European jurisdictions.

The FCC's submarine cable landing license process has faced criticism for delays, particularly as the volume of applications has increased with hyperscaler-funded systems. Environmental reviews under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) and Coastal Zone Management Act (CZMA) add sequential approval stages that cannot be parallelized.

Landing station concentration is itself a vulnerability. Virginia Beach, for example, serves as the US landing point for more than a dozen transatlantic cables. A single facility disruption or permitting moratorium at a concentrated landing point would affect multiple cable systems simultaneously.

## Bottleneck 04 — HVDC power components for submarine power cables

While telecom submarine cables carry their own DC power for repeaters, the growing submarine power cable market (offshore wind, island interconnections) competes for some of the same manufacturing capacity and vessel time. HVDC converter stations — the termination points for submarine power cables — have lead times of 36+ months, with the market dominated by Hitachi Energy, Siemens Energy, and GE Vernova.

The convergence of submarine telecom and submarine power cable demand creates resource competition at the vessel, factory, and engineering talent levels. Prysmian's dual position in both markets (telecom and power submarine cables) means its factory scheduling must balance both order books.

## Terrestrial deployment bottlenecks

For terrestrial fiber cable, the bottlenecks have shifted downstream from manufacturing to installation. Trained fiber splicing crews are the scarcest resource in the US deployment chain. An experienced fiber splicer takes 2–3 years to train, and the workforce pipeline was depleted during the post-2020 telecom spending downturn. As BEAD funding begins flowing into construction in 2026, the competition for qualified crews will intensify.

Conduit and right-of-way access creates additional delays. Municipal permitting for fiber construction varies dramatically by jurisdiction — from weeks in rural areas with expedited processes to 12+ months in dense urban environments with complex utility coordination requirements.

## Takeaway

The subsystem layer's bottlenecks are characterized by irreducible lead times. Wet-plant manufacturing cannot be accelerated below 24–30 months. Vessel scheduling requires 12–18 month advance booking. Permitting follows regulatory timelines immune to market pressure. These bottlenecks interact multiplicatively: a 3-month permitting delay pushes vessel scheduling, which cascades to the next project in the queue. The companies that control these bottleneck assets — SubCom, ASN, NEC for wet-plant; Prysmian for integrated vessel-factory capacity — hold structural pricing power that persists regardless of demand fluctuations.
