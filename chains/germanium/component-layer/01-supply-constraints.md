## The compound

Germanium tetrachloride (GeCl₄) is the critical precursor chemical used to dope silica glass with germanium dioxide (GeO₂) during fiber preform manufacturing. When GeCl₄ vapor is oxidized at 2,000–2,100°C inside a rotating silica tube or on a rotating mandrel, it converts to GeO₂ nanoparticles that are deposited layer by layer into the preform core. This GeO₂ doping raises the refractive index of the fiber core by approximately 0.35%, creating the waveguide effect that allows light to propagate through the fiber.

**There is no alternative dopant for standard single-mode telecommunications fiber.** Fluorine doping lowers refractive index (useful only for cladding), phosphorus serves niche Raman applications, and hollow-core fiber eliminates the need for doping entirely — but none can substitute for germanium in the G.652.D fiber that carries 99%+ of the world's data today.

## Global production

The GeCl₄ market for optical fiber applications was valued at approximately $92 million in 2025 and is projected to reach $104 million by 2033 (CAGR 6.3%). This modest dollar figure belies its strategic importance: $92 million of GeCl₄ enables a fiber optic market worth tens of billions.

Total global germanium production is approximately 220 tonnes per year. Of this, fiber optics consumes roughly 35–44% (77–97 tonnes equivalent), making it the single largest end use. At current deployment rates, the US alone consumes approximately 12 metric tonnes of germanium per year for fiber. If deployment doubles by 2030 as projected for AI infrastructure, approximately 180 tonnes per year would be needed for fiber alone — representing 74–75% of current total global production dedicated to a single application.

## Purity requirements

The purity demands for GeCl₄ vary dramatically by end use, and the highest-value application — fiber optics — requires the most extreme specifications:

**Fiber optics (6N+ / 99.9999%):** The GeCl₄ fed into MCVD, OVD, or VAD deposition systems must contain fewer than 1 part per million of metallic impurities. Arsenic contamination is historically the most difficult impurity to eliminate, as AsCl₃ co-distills with GeCl₄ and must be removed through multi-stage fractional distillation in quartz rectifying towers operating at 75–95°C with 4:1 to 14:1 reflux ratios. A single part-per-billion of arsenic can degrade fiber attenuation from the target of <0.18 dB/km at 1550 nm to commercially unacceptable levels.

**IR optics (4N–5N / 99.99–99.999%):** GeO₂ blanks for infrared windows and lenses require high purity but not the extreme 6N+ specifications of fiber. These serve military thermal imaging, night vision, and FLIR applications.

**Solar cells and catalysts (3N–4N):** Lower-grade GeO₂ for PET catalyst applications and multi-junction solar cell substrates. Commercially available from multiple suppliers.

**The purity gradient creates a pricing pyramid:** 6N+ GeCl₄ commands multiples of the price of lower-grade material, and the number of facilities capable of producing it narrows dramatically at each purity tier.

## Conversion yields and losses

The conversion path from raw germanium to deployed fiber involves multiple yield-loss steps:

**Step 1 — Germanium extraction from zinc ore:** Only about 10% of germanium flowing through zinc smelters is actually captured. Source metal streams contain an estimated 12,000 tonnes of Ge per year globally, but actual recovery is only ~220 tonnes. Less than 3% of germanium in zinc ores and fly ash is recovered worldwide.

**Step 2 — Chlorination (GeO₂ → GeCl₄):** Germanium dioxide is dissolved in concentrated HCl to produce GeCl₄ via: GeO₂ + 4HCl → GeCl₄ + 2H₂O. Industrial chloride technology achieves ≤70% overall germanium yield, with losses occurring in chloride sewage and hydrolysis stages.

**Step 3 — Purification to 6N+:** Multi-stage fractional distillation with HCl and oxidizing agents. Each distillation pass improves purity but incurs material losses. The arsenic removal step alone can reduce yield by 5–10%.

**Step 4 — Deposition in preform manufacturing:** The MCVD process converts GeCl₄ to GeO₂ at 40–60% conversion efficiency under equilibrium conditions. PCVD technology approaches 100% deposition efficiency but is less widely deployed. In standard MCVD, 40–60% of the germanium fed into the system is deposited into the preform; the remainder must be captured and recycled from the exhaust.

**Step 5 — Scrap recovery:** Leading manufacturers recover >95% of unreacted germanium through gas scrubbers and recirculation systems. Approximately 20% of germanium dopant at advanced facilities is sourced from reclaimed preforms.

**Cumulative yield from ore to deployed fiber is estimated at 2–5%.** This extraordinary inefficiency means that expanding fiber output requires vastly more raw germanium than the fiber-kilometers deployed would suggest.

## Capacity constraints

Six structural factors limit GeCl₄ production expansion:

**01 — Byproduct dependency propagates through the component layer.** GeCl₄ production begins with germanium metal, which begins with zinc smelting. No amount of GeCl₄ demand can increase germanium extraction if zinc production is flat or declining.

**02 — Purification infrastructure is scarce and capital-intensive.** Building a new 6N+ GeCl₄ distillation facility requires specialized quartz equipment, cleanroom environments, and process know-how accumulated over decades. Lead time from investment decision to first commercial output is 3–5 years minimum.

**03 — The preform bottleneck amplifies the GeCl₄ constraint.** Even if more GeCl₄ were available, preform manufacturing capacity is running at 100% utilization with an 18–24 month expansion cycle. New preform lines require more GeCl₄, creating a demand pull that tightens the very supply they depend on.

**04 — Price escalation reflects structural scarcity.** GeO₂ prices rose from €940/kg in January 2024 to €2,125/kg by September 2024. Germanium metal climbed from €1,550/kg to €2,950/kg in the same period, and continued to $8,597/kg by February 2026. These are not cyclical price swings — they reflect a market where supply cannot respond to demand signals.

**05 — Recycling has limits at the component layer.** Umicore recycles fiber manufacturing scrap back into GeCl₄, but fiber optics is the largest end use and germanium doped into deployed cable is non-recoverable. The recycling loop only captures manufacturing waste, not the product itself.

**06 — Military demand is absorbing high-purity supply.** Military orders for fiber-guided munitions and IR optics have been given priority access to the highest-purity preforms, creating shortages for commercial cable manufacturers. G.657A1 fiber prices spiked to $22/km and G.657A2 to $35/km in the 2025–2026 period.

## Takeaway

The GeO₂/GeCl₄ component layer is where the germanium supply chain's constraints become acute. Raw germanium scarcity is the underlying cause, but the conversion from metal to ultrapure chemical compound introduces additional yield losses, capacity bottlenecks, and concentration risks that amplify the shortage at every step. Western fiber manufacturers are dependent on a single supplier (Umicore) for a single chemical compound (6N+ GeCl₄) that no other western facility produces at scale.

**If germanium is the strategic material, GeCl₄ is the strategic chokepoint.**
