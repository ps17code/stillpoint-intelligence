# Bottlenecks — GeO₂ / GeCl₄ Component Layer

## Teaser
Six facilities, one equipment supplier, and a 40–60% deposition yield stand between raw germanium and every fiber-km the world produces

## Title
The component layer has five chokepoints — and three of them run through the same company

## Category
Bottlenecks

## Overview

Supply constraints explain why there isn't enough germanium to meet demand. Bottlenecks answer a different question: **where does the existing component-layer supply chain break if a single node fails?** The conversion of raw germanium into optical-grade GeCl₄ and then into fiber preforms contains five critical chokepoints — each capable of halting western fiber production independently.

## Bottleneck 01 — Umicore: sole western GeCl₄ producer at scale

**Olen, Belgium · ~35% global GeCl₄ market share · >50% from recycled feed**

Severity: **95% — Critical**

Every western fiber preform manufacturer — Corning, Prysmian, Sumitomo, Fujikura — sources ultrapure GeCl₄ from Umicore. Their Olen facility near Antwerp is the only non-Chinese, non-Russian operation producing 6N+ purity GeCl₄ at volumes sufficient to supply the western fiber industry. Umicore constitutes approximately 35% of the world's GeCl₄ market, with the highest purity production standards in the industry.

Umicore's position is reinforced by vertical integration: they refine raw germanium concentrates, purify through multi-stage distillation, and produce finished GeCl₄ at a single integrated site. More than 50% of their germanium feed comes from recycling manufacturing scrap — a feedstock secured through long-term tolling agreements with the same fiber manufacturers they supply.

**If Olen experiences any disruption — regulatory, operational, labor, or feedstock — there is no western alternative at comparable scale or purity.** The next-largest non-Chinese producer (5N Plus in Canada) is still ramping toward 20 tonnes per year under DoD funding, with meaningful output not expected before 2027–2028.

Prysmian Group renewed its long-term germanium supply agreement with Umicore in 2025, explicitly committing to 100% sustainable germanium through recycling initiatives. This contract locks Prysmian into the Umicore dependency — and simultaneously confirms that no alternative supplier exists.

## Bottleneck 02 — Rosendahl Nextrom: near-monopoly on preform deposition equipment

**Austria (KNILL Gruppe) · Hundreds of MCVD units since 1990 · Projects in 73+ countries**

Severity: **80% — High**

The equipment used to convert GeCl₄ into GeO₂-doped preforms is manufactured by a remarkably small number of companies. Rosendahl Nextrom's OFC 12 MCVD system is described as "the most widely used MCVD system in the industry, with hundreds of units delivered since 1990." They supply complete preform production lines including MCVD, PCVD, VAD, and OVD systems, plus sleeving lathes, collapsing equipment, and fiber draw towers.

The remaining equipment suppliers — Heraeus Covantics (fused silica substrate tubes), SG Controls (Cambridge, UK), and FCJ Opto Tech — serve narrower niches. Fewer than 10 production groups globally manufacture fiber preform equipment, concentrated across North America, Europe, and Asia.

**This is a hidden bottleneck.** If a fiber manufacturer wants to expand preform capacity, the 18–24 month timeline includes equipment procurement from this concentrated supplier base. A surge in simultaneous expansion orders (which the current demand environment is producing) creates equipment delivery backlogs that extend the capacity ramp beyond even the inherent process timeline.

Nextrom is privately held (Austrian family-owned since 1712). There is no public market signal when their order book extends — the bottleneck is invisible until it manifests as delayed capacity expansions at Corning, Prysmian, or YOFC.

## Bottleneck 03 — 6N+ purification: arsenic removal as the binding constraint

**~5–6 facilities globally · Multi-stage quartz distillation · Arsenic co-distillation problem**

Severity: **75% — High**

Producing optical-grade GeCl₄ at 6N+ purity (99.9999%) is not a commodity chemical process. The binding technical challenge is removing arsenic: AsCl₃ has a boiling point (130°C) close to GeCl₄ (83.1°C), requiring specialized extraction with HCl and chlorine in quartz stills followed by repeated fractional distillation.

Only approximately 5–6 major facilities globally can produce 6N+ GeCl₄: Umicore (Belgium), JSC Germanium (Russia, sanctioned), Shin-Etsu Chemical (Japan), Yunnan Chihong (China), possibly Indium Corporation (US, small scale), and GRINM/Vital Materials (China). Of these, only Umicore is accessible to western fiber manufacturers without geopolitical constraint.

Standard chemical distillation equipment is inadequate for 6N+ purity — quartz rectifying towers with precise temperature control (75–95°C column temperature, 4:1 to 14:1 reflux ratios) are required. Building new purification capacity requires not just capital but accumulated process know-how that takes years to develop.

**The purification bottleneck means that even if more raw germanium became available tomorrow, converting it to fiber-grade GeCl₄ would take years of facility construction and process optimization.**

## Bottleneck 04 — Preform manufacturing concentration: top 3 control >40% of capacity

**YOFC (3,500 t/a) · Corning (~15.5% share) · Prysmian · 18–24 month expansion cycle**

Severity: **70% — High**

Global fiber preform manufacturing is concentrated among approximately 20 companies, with the top three — YOFC, Corning, and Prysmian — controlling over 40% of combined capacity (as of 2019 data; concentration has likely increased). All preform lines globally are running at 95–100% utilization.

The 18–24 month expansion timeline for preform capacity is irreducible. It reflects the physics of building and commissioning MCVD/OVD/VAD systems, not a lack of capital or intent. Even after equipment installation, yield optimization can take several additional quarters before a new line reaches full commercial output.

Every new preform line commissioned increases GeCl₄ demand. The preform bottleneck and the GeCl₄ bottleneck are coupled: expanding one tightens the other. This creates a feedback loop where capacity relief at the preform layer immediately becomes demand pressure at the GeCl₄ layer.

Chinese G.652.D fiber prices advanced 80%+ between November 2025 and January 2026, reaching 31.5 yuan per core-km — with some transactions at 40–50 yuan (94–144% cumulative gains). These prices reflect preform scarcity that cannot be resolved before late 2027 at earliest.

## Bottleneck 05 — Military demand priority: high-purity preforms redirected from commercial supply

**G.657A1 at $22/km · G.657A2 at $35/km · Military orders given priority allocation**

Severity: **60% — Medium**

A bottleneck that did not exist 24 months ago: fiber-guided munitions (FPV drones consuming 10–40 km of single-mode fiber per flight) have created an entirely new demand category pulling from the same preform pool as commercial fiber. Military orders have been given priority access to the highest-purity preforms needed for low-attenuation G.657 fibers, creating shortages for commercial cable manufacturers.

Russia's fiber procurement alone jumped from less than 1% to 10.5% of global fiber demand in 2025. This demand is disposable — every fiber-guided flight consumes the fiber permanently — and is not price-sensitive. Defense procurement operates outside commercial supply-demand equilibrium, creating an extraction of high-purity capacity that commercial buyers cannot outbid.

## Compounding risk

Umicore is the sole western GeCl₄ producer (Bottleneck #1), AND depends on preform manufacturers' scrap for recycled feed (coupled to #4), AND supplies GeCl₄ to the same preform manufacturers whose military-priority production reduces Umicore's recycling feedstock (#5).

**A single company sits at the intersection of three of the five identified bottlenecks. The component layer's resilience is, in practice, Umicore's resilience.**
