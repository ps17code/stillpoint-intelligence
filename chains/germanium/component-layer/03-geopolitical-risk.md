# Geopolitical Risk — GeO₂ / GeCl₄ Component Layer

## Teaser
China didn't just control germanium metal — it explicitly listed GeO₂ and GeCl₄ as separate controlled items from day one

## Title
The export controls were designed for the component layer — GeO₂ and GeCl₄ were named specifically, not caught incidentally

## Category
Geopolitical risk

---

## Overview

When MOFCOM announced export controls on germanium in July 2023, the policy was not limited to raw germanium metal. **Six specific germanium products were individually enumerated** — and GeO₂ and GeCl₄ were both explicitly named as controlled items. This was not an oversight or broad categorization. China designed the controls to target the component layer directly, recognizing that controlling the chemical intermediates is more strategically effective than controlling the metal alone.

## The six controlled items

MOFCOM's July 3, 2023 announcement listed the following items subject to export licensing, effective August 1, 2023:

1. Metal germanium
2. Zone-melted germanium ingots
3. Phosphorus germanium zinc (GeZnP)
4. Germanium epitaxial growth substrates
5. **Germanium dioxide (GeO₂)** — HS code 2825.60
6. **Germanium tetrachloride (GeCl₄)** — classified under dual-use export control regulations

GeO₂ and GeCl₄ are classified under separate HS codes from germanium metal (which uses 2804.50 or 8112.99 depending on form). This means they are tracked, licensed, and controlled independently at the customs level. **A company importing germanium metal and converting it to GeCl₄ domestically does not bypass the controls — the finished compound is itself a controlled item requiring a separate MOFCOM license for export.**

## Component-level impact is more severe than raw material controls

The component-layer controls are strategically more damaging to western fiber manufacturers than raw metal controls for three reasons:

**01 — GeCl₄ has fewer substitution pathways than germanium metal.** Raw germanium has multiple end uses (IR optics, solar, electronics, fiber). GeCl₄ is purpose-built for fiber preform manufacturing. Controlling GeCl₄ directly targets the fiber optic supply chain with surgical precision.

**02 — Licensing approval is slower for dual-use chemicals.** GeCl₄ is classified as a dual-use chemical compound, requiring end-user documentation and intended-use verification from MOFCOM. Each application is reviewed independently. The licensing process introduces unpredictable delays that prevent reliable supply planning — even when licenses are eventually granted.

**03 — Third-country routing is harder for processed chemicals.** The Stimson Center documented that Chinese germanium exports to Belgium increased approximately 224% in 2024 relative to 2022, suggesting third-country routing of germanium metal. But GeCl₄ re-export is technically riskier: the compound carries specific chemical identification that can be traced to its origin, and the licensing system requires end-use documentation that creates paper trails.

## China's domestic GeCl₄ producers

Three major Chinese entities produce GeCl₄ for the domestic fiber optic market:

**Yunnan Chihong Zinc & Germanium (Shanghai: 600497):** State-owned subsidiary of Chinalco (Aluminum Corporation of China). Produced 65.9 tonnes of germanium products in 2023 and operates a dedicated GeCl₄ production line with 30 metric tonnes per year capacity specifically for optical fiber applications. This single production line represents a meaningful fraction of global ultrapure GeCl₄ supply.

**China Germanium (Nanjing):** Located in Nanjing Lishui Economic Development Zone. Production capacity of 25 tonnes/year germanium ingot, 15 tonnes/year GeO₂. Produces GeCl₄ for fiber optic and infrared applications. Export-dependent company now subject to mandatory MOFCOM licensing.

**GRINM / Vital Materials:** State-linked germanium processor serving domestic supply chain. Capacity undisclosed.

All three are subject to mandatory export licensing since August 1, 2023. Yunnan Chihong, as a state-owned entity under Chinalco, faces additional political pressure to prioritize domestic consumption and allied-nation supply relationships. **Chinese fiber manufacturers (YOFC, Hengtong, FiberHome) access GeCl₄ from these domestic producers through a parallel supply chain that is completely insulated from export controls.**

## Impact on western fiber manufacturers

The export controls have produced measurable effects at the component layer:

**Supply disruption:** Companies using Chinese-sourced GeCl₄ face unpredictable licensing approval timelines. MOFCOM reviews each application independently, creating planning uncertainty that is incompatible with the 18–24 month preform production cycles that fiber manufacturers operate on.

**Cost premium:** Secondary sourcing from Umicore (the only non-Chinese option at scale) adds 15–25% cost premiums relative to pre-control Chinese pricing. Germanium metal prices climbed 200% from January 2024 to February 2026, and GeCl₄ pricing followed.

**Contractual lock-in:** Prysmian's 2025 renewal of its Umicore supply agreement signals that western manufacturers see no near-term alternative to single-source dependency. They are contractually codifying the bottleneck rather than diversifying away from it.

**YOFC's structural advantage:** Chinese fiber manufacturers source GeCl₄ domestically from Yunnan Chihong and state chemical plants — completely insulated from export controls. YOFC and Chinese peers operate in a parallel supply chain immune to the constraints affecting Corning and Prysmian. This is not a temporary trade disruption — it is a permanent structural cost and availability advantage for Chinese fiber manufacturers.

## The November 2025 suspension

Following a Trump-Xi meeting in Busan, MOFCOM suspended the full US export ban (Announcement No. 46, December 2024) effective November 9, 2025. The suspension runs until November 27, 2026.

**What the suspension covers:** The outright prohibition on germanium exports to US civilian end users was lifted. GeCl₄ falls under the suspended ban — meaning US fiber manufacturers may receive MOFCOM export licenses for GeCl₄ during the suspension period.

**What the suspension does NOT cover:** The global dual-use export licensing requirement (August 2023) remains in full force. Military end-user restrictions remain fully in effect. MOFCOM retains discretion to grant or deny individual export licenses. **The suspension does not restore free trade — it reverts to the standard dual-use export control regime.**

**What this means for GeCl₄:** The suspension provides partial relief for civilian fiber manufacturers, but does not eliminate the licensing bottleneck. Every GeCl₄ shipment still requires MOFCOM approval with end-use documentation. The control architecture remains intact, and China retains the ability to reimpose the full ban after November 27, 2026 with a single announcement.

## Enforcement escalation

China is actively closing loopholes in its export control enforcement:

**May 2025:** China launched a coordinated interagency crackdown on transshipment and smuggling involving 10+ central ministries. Belgium and other conduit countries were explicitly targeted in enforcement messaging.

**Long-arm jurisdiction:** The December 2024 ban invoked "long-arm jurisdiction" provisions — any organization globally violating the rules faces legal consequences. This is designed to prevent third-country processors from re-exporting Chinese-origin GeCl₄ to US end users.

## Bottom line

The geopolitical risk at the component layer is more acute than at the raw material layer for a simple reason: **China designed the controls specifically to include GeO₂ and GeCl₄ as named items, controls them under stricter dual-use chemical licensing than raw metal, and Chinese fiber manufacturers operate in a parallel supply chain that is immune to the very restrictions they impose on western competitors.**

The November 2025 suspension is a diplomatic gesture, not a structural resolution. The licensing regime remains. MOFCOM discretion remains. The expiration date remains. Any western company building its GeCl₄ supply strategy around resumed Chinese access is building on a foundation that disappears on November 27, 2026.
