## Overview

The end-use layer is the most geopolitically visible segment of the germanium supply chain. While germanium export controls and submarine cable routing involve specialized policy communities, datacenter investment decisions are made at the head-of-state level. The $660–690 billion in hyperscaler capex planned for 2026 is not purely a commercial allocation — it reflects and reinforces geopolitical alignments that will shape the global AI infrastructure landscape for decades.

## NVIDIA export controls and the AI Diffusion Rule

The US AI Diffusion Rule (effective May 2025) established a three-tier country classification system for AI chip exports. Tier 1 countries (close allies — Japan, UK, Australia, EU members) face minimal restrictions. Tier 2 countries (most of the world) face annual caps on compute imports. Tier 3 countries (China, Russia, and designated arms-embargoed nations) face near-total prohibition.

NVIDIA's Blackwell architecture — the B100, B200, and GB200 NVL72 systems — is entirely controlled for export. Unlike the previous H100/A100 generation, where "de-featured" variants (H800, A800) were sold to China before the October 2023 controls, no Blackwell variant is authorized for Tier 3 export. This makes the GPU allocation decision an implicitly geopolitical one: every chip shipped to a Tier 1 or Tier 2 country is a chip unavailable for any other destination.

The export control regime has created a parallel market in computing capacity. Chinese AI companies (Baidu, Alibaba, ByteDance, Tencent) are developing domestic alternatives (Huawei Ascend, Biren Technology, Cambricon), renting cloud compute from intermediaries in Tier 2 countries, and stockpiling pre-restriction chips. The effectiveness of the controls is debated, but the structural impact on datacenter investment is clear: China is building a domestically sourced AI infrastructure stack, while the rest of the world depends on US-controlled supply chains.

## Sovereign AI and Gulf state investment

The Gulf states — particularly the UAE and Saudi Arabia — have emerged as the fastest-growing market for datacenter investment, driven by sovereign AI ambitions and the financial capacity to execute at scale.

**UAE — G42 and Microsoft:** G42, the Abu Dhabi-based AI company backed by the Mubadala sovereign wealth fund, has committed $15.2 billion in partnership with Microsoft for datacenter infrastructure in the UAE. This includes Azure cloud regions, AI training clusters, and supporting power infrastructure. The partnership required G42 to divest its Chinese technology relationships (particularly its use of Huawei equipment) as a condition of US government approval.

**Saudi Arabia — NEOM and DataVolt:** The $5 billion DataVolt project in NEOM represents Saudi Arabia's entry into hyperscale datacenter infrastructure. Powered by renewable energy (solar and wind), DataVolt is positioned as a sustainable AI infrastructure play aligned with Saudi Vision 2030 diversification goals.

**Qatar, Bahrain, Oman:** Smaller Gulf states are pursuing datacenter capacity as both infrastructure for domestic digital economies and revenue-generating assets that attract global cloud providers. Oracle, AWS, and Google have all announced or expanded Gulf region datacenter presence.

The geopolitical dimension: Gulf sovereign AI investments require access to NVIDIA GPUs (Tier 2 country allocation), US technology partnerships (subject to CFIUS and export license review), and submarine cable connectivity (the subsystem layer). Each dependency creates leverage for the US and potential friction with Gulf states' desire for technology sovereignty.

## Data sovereignty and regulatory fragmentation

Data sovereignty laws are fragmenting the global datacenter market into regulatory islands where data must be stored and processed within national borders. This fragmentation drives demand for local datacenter capacity in every regulated jurisdiction, but constrains the economies of scale that make hyperscale datacenters efficient.

**European Union — GDPR and the AI Act:** GDPR data residency requirements have driven massive datacenter expansion across EU member states. The EU AI Act (effective 2026) adds compute governance requirements for high-risk AI systems, potentially mandating that certain AI workloads run on EU-based infrastructure with EU-controlled audit access.

**India — DPDP Act:** The Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023) establishes data localization requirements that are driving datacenter investment in Mumbai, Chennai, and Hyderabad. India's datacenter market is projected to grow from 1.3 GW to 3+ GW by 2028, with Adani, Reliance, and Tata competing alongside global operators.

**Indonesia, Brazil, Nigeria:** Emerging markets are implementing data sovereignty laws that require local data processing, creating demand for in-country datacenter capacity but limiting the ability of hyperscalers to serve these markets from regional hubs.

The regulatory fragmentation means that a hyperscaler cannot serve 50 countries from 5 mega-datacenters — it needs presence in 30+ jurisdictions, each with local power, construction, and connectivity requirements. This multiplies the end-use layer's demand for fiber connectivity (the germanium supply chain) across every regulated market.

## AUKUS datacenter cooperation

The AUKUS security pact (Australia, UK, US) has expanded beyond nuclear submarine technology to include AI and quantum computing infrastructure cooperation. AUKUS Pillar II includes provisions for shared AI research infrastructure, interoperable classified cloud environments, and joint datacenter development for defense AI workloads.

Australia's AUCloud and UK's UKCloud provide sovereign cloud services to AUKUS defense ministries. The expansion of classified datacenter capacity in Australia (Pine Gap adjacent, Sydney, Melbourne) and the UK (Cheltenham/GCHQ corridor) is driven by AUKUS cooperation requirements. These facilities require the highest-assurance fiber connectivity — another demand vector for the germanium supply chain.

## China's domestic datacenter buildout

China's "East Data, West Computation" (东数西算) initiative directs datacenter construction toward western provinces (Guizhou, Gansu, Inner Mongolia) where land and power are cheaper. The initiative aims to build 8 national computing hub nodes and 10 national datacenter clusters by 2025.

China's domestic datacenter market is expected to exceed 40 GW by 2030. Critically, this buildout uses domestically manufactured fiber (from YOFC, Hengtong, FiberHome), domestically produced servers (from Inspur, Sugon, Huawei), and domestically developed AI chips (Huawei Ascend 910C/D). The only western dependency that China has not fully replaced is the EUV lithography equipment (ASML) required for leading-edge chip manufacturing — which limits domestic AI chip performance to approximately 2 generations behind NVIDIA's frontier.

For the germanium supply chain, China's domestic buildout is significant because it represents a parallel demand stream for germanium-doped fiber that is entirely outside western supply chain visibility. If MOFCOM lifts germanium export controls on November 27, 2026 (the current suspension expiry), the released supply would serve both western and Chinese fiber demand — reducing the net relief to western supply chains.

## Takeaway

The end-use layer's geopolitical risks are simultaneously the most visible (GPU export controls generate front-page headlines) and the most consequential for the germanium supply chain. Data sovereignty laws multiply global datacenter demand and fiber connectivity requirements. Sovereign AI investments in the Gulf, India, and ASEAN create new demand centers with their own power, construction, and connectivity needs. The bifurcation between US-aligned and Chinese-aligned infrastructure stacks means that the germanium supply chain serves two parallel markets that are increasingly disconnected at every layer.
