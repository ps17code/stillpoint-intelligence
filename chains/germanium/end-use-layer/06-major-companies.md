# Major Companies — AI Datacenter End-Use Layer

## Teaser
Equinix is doubling capacity by 2029, Digital Realty's Digital Dulles covers 11.7M sq ft, Dycom's $9.54B backlog deploys the fiber, and Turner Construction builds 37% of all US datacenter shells — seven companies that define the end-use layer's physical reality

## Title
Seven companies control the end-use layer's critical infrastructure: Equinix and Digital Realty (colocation/wholesale), Dycom (fiber deployment), Turner Construction (datacenter construction), Vertiv (cooling and power), Corning (fiber manufacturing), and Oracle (Stargate and sovereign cloud)

## Overview

The end-use layer's competitive landscape is broader than upstream layers because datacenters require diverse infrastructure — real estate, power, cooling, fiber, construction, and IT equipment — each controlled by different companies. The companies profiled here are not the hyperscalers themselves (Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft) but the infrastructure providers that the hyperscalers depend on. These are the companies that translate hyperscaler capex into physical facilities, and they are the primary beneficiaries of the $660–690 billion investment wave.

## Equinix (NASDAQ: EQIX) — The interconnection platform

**Market cap:** ~$85 billion
**Revenue:** ~$8.7 billion (FY2025 annualized)
**Global footprint:** 260+ datacenters in 72 metro markets across 33 countries
**Key metric:** 10,000+ customers, 500,000+ cross-connects

Equinix is the world's largest datacenter REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust) and the dominant provider of interconnection-dense colocation. Unlike wholesale datacenter operators that sell raw power and space, Equinix's value proposition centers on network density — the more networks, cloud providers, and enterprises that colocate in an Equinix facility, the more valuable each connection becomes.

Equinix is doubling its datacenter capacity by 2029 through a combination of organic development and xScale joint ventures (partnerships with sovereign wealth funds and infrastructure investors that provide capital for hyperscale builds). The xScale program targets purpose-built hyperscale facilities adjacent to Equinix's retail colocation campuses, creating a hybrid model that serves both interconnection customers and hyperscale tenants.

**Competitive position:** Equinix's interconnection network effect is its primary moat — no competitor matches its geographic breadth or customer density. The key risk is the AI-driven shift toward fewer, larger facilities that favor wholesale operators over interconnection-focused colocation. Equinix's xScale strategy addresses this but dilutes the interconnection premium.

## Digital Realty (NYSE: DLR) — The wholesale hyperscale platform

**Market cap:** ~$55 billion
**Revenue:** ~$5.8 billion (FY2025 annualized)
**Global footprint:** 300+ datacenters across 50+ metro markets, 28 countries
**Key asset:** Digital Dulles campus — 11.7 million square feet, Northern Virginia

Digital Realty is the largest wholesale datacenter provider, specializing in large-scale leases (1 MW+) to hyperscalers, cloud providers, and large enterprises. The Digital Dulles campus in Northern Virginia is the company's flagship development — at 11.7 million square feet, it is among the largest datacenter campuses in the world.

Digital Realty's competitive strategy emphasizes powered shell delivery — pre-built datacenter buildings with utility power and cooling infrastructure that hyperscaler tenants complete with their own IT equipment. This model reduces deployment timelines versus build-to-suit construction.

**Competitive position:** Digital Realty competes primarily on land bank (buildable campus sites with power access), speed of delivery, and pricing. The company's concentrated exposure to Northern Virginia (its largest market) creates both opportunity (highest demand) and risk (power constraints, political backlash).

## Dycom Industries (NYSE: DY) — Fiber deployment at scale

**Market cap:** ~$17 billion
**Revenue:** $5.55 billion (FY2026)
**Backlog:** $9.54 billion
**FY2027 guidance:** $6.85–7.15 billion
**Employees:** ~15,000

Dycom appears in both the subsystem and end-use layers because its terrestrial fiber deployment serves both long-haul/metro networks (subsystem layer) and datacenter campus connectivity (end-use layer). Dycom's crews install the fiber that connects datacenters to the network — the final physical link in the germanium supply chain.

The $9.54 billion backlog reflects contracts from telcos (AT&T, Lumen, Verizon), cable operators, and hyperscalers. As hyperscalers build campus fiber networks connecting multiple buildings within datacenter campuses, Dycom's work shifts from traditional telecom construction toward datacenter-adjacent fiber deployment.

**Competitive position:** Identical to the subsystem layer assessment — Dycom's labor moat (largest trained fiber workforce in North America) is the scarcest resource in the deployment chain.

## Turner Construction Company — Datacenter construction dominance

**Ownership:** Subsidiary of HOCHTIEF AG (public, Germany), which is majority-owned by ACS Group (public, Spain)
**Backlog:** $44.3 billion total, ~37% datacenter ($16+ billion)
**Datacenter revenue:** ~$9 billion
**Key clients:** Meta, Google, Microsoft, Amazon

Turner Construction is the largest commercial builder in the United States and the dominant datacenter construction contractor. The company's datacenter practice has grown from a small specialty group to its largest market segment, reflecting the explosion in hyperscaler construction demand.

Turner's competitive advantage is execution at scale — the ability to manage 10+ simultaneous datacenter construction projects across multiple states, each requiring coordination of 500+ workers, precision mechanical/electrical systems, and aggressive schedules. Turner's safety record and union labor relationships give it access to the most experienced construction workforce.

**Competitive position:** Turner's dominance creates single-contractor risk for the industry. DPR Construction, Holder Construction, and Fortis Construction compete but at smaller scale. The construction labor shortage limits all contractors equally — Turner's advantage is in workforce access and management, not in immunity from the labor constraint.

## Vertiv Holdings (NYSE: VRT) — Critical infrastructure and cooling

**Market cap:** ~$45 billion
**Revenue:** ~$8 billion (FY2025 annualized)
**Backlog:** ~$15 billion
**Key products:** UPS systems, thermal management (liquid cooling, precision air), power distribution, monitoring

Vertiv is the leading provider of critical infrastructure for datacenters — the power and cooling systems that keep servers running. The company's product portfolio spans uninterruptible power supplies (UPS), precision cooling (air and liquid), power distribution units (PDUs), and infrastructure monitoring software.

The $15 billion backlog reflects the AI-driven surge in thermal management demand. Vertiv's liquid cooling product line — including direct-to-chip cooling systems designed for NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 racks — positions the company at the intersection of the AI compute and datacenter infrastructure markets.

**Competitive position:** Vertiv's installed base (the existing UPS and cooling systems in thousands of datacenters globally) creates replacement and upgrade revenue streams. The transition to liquid cooling represents a generational product cycle that benefits Vertiv's engineering depth. Schneider Electric and Eaton compete in UPS; CoolIT and Asetek compete in liquid cooling; but Vertiv's integrated portfolio (power + cooling + monitoring) is unique.

## Corning Incorporated (NYSE: GLW) — Fiber manufacturing backbone

**Market cap:** ~$40 billion
**Revenue:** ~$14 billion (FY2025)
**Key agreement:** $6 billion multi-year fiber supply agreement with Meta (January 2026)
**Key facility:** Hickory, North Carolina (world's largest optical fiber manufacturing complex)

Corning is the world's largest manufacturer of optical fiber and cable, producing both preforms and finished fiber at its Hickory complex. The $6 billion Meta agreement — the largest single fiber supply contract in history — provides revenue visibility and justifies capacity expansion.

Corning's competitive position in the germanium supply chain is unique: it is both a consumer of GeCl₄ (for preform manufacturing) and a supplier of fiber to the end-use layer. The company's BABA-compliant US manufacturing operations position it to capture BEAD-funded demand alongside hyperscaler campus and metro fiber needs.

**Competitive position:** Corning's technology leadership in fiber manufacturing (low-loss fiber, bend-insensitive fiber, high-count cables) and its integrated preform-to-fiber production create barriers to entry. YOFC (China) and Prysmian compete globally but Corning dominates the North American market.

## Oracle Corporation (NYSE: ORCL) — Stargate and sovereign cloud

**Market cap:** ~$350 billion
**Cloud revenue:** $25+ billion annualized (OCI growing 50%+ YoY)
**Datacenter footprint:** 147+ active datacenters globally
**Key initiative:** Stargate infrastructure partner ($500B, 7 sites)

Oracle's inclusion in the end-use layer reflects its transformation from a database company into a hyperscale infrastructure provider. Oracle's role as a Stargate infrastructure partner (alongside OpenAI and SoftBank) positions it at the center of the largest datacenter investment commitment in history.

Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI) has differentiated on price-performance for AI workloads, winning customers that find AWS, Azure, and GCP pricing prohibitive for large-scale AI training. Oracle's rapid datacenter expansion — from 40 regions to 147+ datacenters in 3 years — demonstrates construction and deployment velocity that exceeds larger competitors.

**Competitive position:** Oracle's position is unconventional. It lacks the customer base of AWS/Azure/GCP but has construction velocity, Stargate partnership positioning, and Larry Ellison's personal relationships with the AI leadership ecosystem (OpenAI, xAI). The risk is execution at Stargate scale — $500 billion in committed investment requires a level of construction and power delivery that no company has previously achieved.

## Takeaway

The end-use layer's company landscape reflects the multi-dimensional nature of datacenter infrastructure. No single company controls the entire stack. Equinix and Digital Realty provide the facilities. Turner Construction builds them. Vertiv powers and cools them. Corning supplies the fiber. Dycom installs it. Oracle operates the cloud platform. This interdependence means that the end-use layer's growth rate is set by the weakest link in this chain — currently power delivery and construction labor — not by the strongest.
